Nitrile garden gloves occupy a strange middle ground in the hand tools category. They're too thin for serious thorn work, too grippy for anything delicate, and yet most gardeners have a drawer full of them because they're cheap and they're there. If you've been defaulting to nitrile out of habit rather than conviction, these two gloves are worth a look as alternatives that actually solve different problems.

Both the [Pine Tree Tools Bamboo Garden Gloves for Women & Men](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B00W5TGD3W?tag=fourseasong-20) and the [Foxgloves Original Gardening Gloves, Purple/Medium](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B006C61Y42?tag=fourseasong-20) sit in the budget range and are machine washable, which is where the similarities end. One is built around breathability and daily comfort. The other is built around coverage. Choosing between them is less about quality and more about what you're actually doing in the garden.

Before we get into the comparison, I'll note that both of these sit within the broader [Hand Tools](/hand-tools/) category on this site, where you'll find context on other glove types, pruners, and related equipment.

## Head-to-Head Verdict

The Pine Tree Tools gloves are the better everyday glove for most gardeners. Breathable, touchscreen-compatible, machine washable, and comfortable through a two-hour weeding session in warm weather. If the bulk of your garden work is planting, weeding, and general soil handling, they'll serve you well for the price.

The Foxgloves gloves are not a replacement for the Pine Tree Tools. They solve a different problem. If you've ever pulled a rose cane back and had it rake across your forearm, or spent a morning harvesting tomatoes in a tank top and ended up with a lattice of scratches from the stems, that's what the Foxgloves address. The extended wrist and forearm coverage is the entire point of this glove, and it's genuinely useful for anyone working with canes, rough bark, or scratchy foliage.

![Nitrile Garden Gloves — photo by ](/images/articles/hand-tools-3.jpg)


My recommendation: buy both. They cost roughly $13 to $20 each at the time of writing, depending on size and current promotions. Using them for different tasks is more sensible than trying to find one glove that does everything.

If you're going to pick only one, ask yourself whether forearm scratches are a real issue in your garden. If not, the Pine Tree Tools gloves are the practical choice.

## Testing Notes

### Pine Tree Tools Bamboo Gloves

I used these through spring planting and a stretch of midsummer weeding in beds where I'm on my knees for an hour or more at a stretch. The bamboo fiber does what it claims. My hands stayed notably drier than they do in standard nitrile, which tends to create a damp, uncomfortable interior within about 20 minutes of warm-weather work. The Pine Tree Tools gloves don't have that problem.

![Nitrile Garden Gloves — photo by ](/images/articles/hand-tools-4.jpg)


The touchscreen compatibility is not a gimmick. I use my phone constantly in the garden, whether for plant ID, timers, or notes, and being able to swipe without pulling a glove off and dropping it in the dirt is a small but real quality-of-life improvement. (I counted: I checked my phone eleven times during one 90-minute session. Removing a glove each time would have been genuinely irritating.)

The fit concern in the product reviews is legitimate. I wear a medium in most gloves, ordered medium, and found them slightly snug. Not unwearable, but if you're between sizes, order up. The bamboo does stretch a little with use.

Durability is reasonable for the price. I've had a pair last about eight months of regular spring-through-fall use before the fingertip coating showed meaningful wear. For a $13 glove, that's acceptable. For comparison, the Showa 370B nitrile gloves I used before these typically lasted about four to five months before the palm grip started flaking.

### Foxgloves Original Gloves

The defining feature is the sleeve extension, which runs several inches past the wrist and stays put. It doesn't bunch or slide down during use, which is not a given with extended-cuff gloves. The spandex blend keeps tension without restricting movement.

The silicone grip pattern works well on tool handles. It's less aggressive than a rubber-coated nitrile palm, which I prefer when I'm doing tasks that require some tactile feedback, like tying in climbers or working with small seedlings. For heavy tool work, you'd want something more substantial, but for the tasks these gloves are designed for, the grip is appropriate.

![Nitrile Garden Gloves — photo by ](/images/articles/hand-tools-5.jpg)


The durability caveat from the specs is real. If you're working on rough surfaces, concrete edging, or abrasive gravel, the silicone wears faster than you'd expect. These are garden gloves, not landscaping gloves. Using them to drag stone pavers or haul bags of grit will shorten their life considerably.

Washing performance is one of the better aspects of this glove. After about twenty machine washes, the spandex blend still holds its shape and the silicone grip is intact. I've had cheaper gloves lose their fit by wash six or seven.

## Who Each Is Best For

### Pine Tree Tools Bamboo Gloves Are Right for You If...

You spend significant time planting or weeding in warm conditions and sweat through standard nitrile gloves. You want a glove you can wear for extended periods without your hands feeling trapped. You use your phone regularly in the garden, or you value the small convenience of not having to remove gloves constantly. You're comfortable with a lower-cost glove and expect to replace it seasonally.

These are also a reasonable choice for gardeners who have mild latex sensitivity and have been using nitrile purely as an avoidance strategy. The bamboo blend is a different material category entirely.

### Foxgloves Original Gloves Are Right for You If...

You work regularly with roses, climbing plants, raspberries, or any cane or bramble growth where the main hazard is forearm scratches rather than deep punctures. You garden in short sleeves and have skin that shows scratches easily, which covers more people than you'd think once you're past your mid-forties. You do a lot of harvesting in dense plantings where foliage contact is constant.

![Nitrile Garden Gloves — photo by ](/images/articles/hand-tools-6.jpg)


The Foxgloves are also useful for gardeners with sun sensitivity who want coverage without gardening in long sleeves in summer heat. The spandex is lightweight enough to be tolerable in warm weather, which a long-sleeved shirt often isn't.

One note on sizing: the Foxgloves product listing on Amazon has multiple ASINs for different size and color combinations. The purple medium is [Foxgloves Original Gardening Gloves, Purple/Medium](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B006C61Y42?tag=fourseasong-20), but if you need a different size, verify the ASIN before ordering. A few reviewers have received the wrong size because they assumed all variants were listed under a single dropdown. They're not always.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are these gloves actually better than standard nitrile garden gloves?

For certain tasks, yes. Nitrile gloves are effective for soil barrier protection and handling pesticides or fertilizers, but they perform poorly in warm weather because they don't breathe. The Pine Tree Tools bamboo gloves are more comfortable for extended use in warm conditions. The Foxgloves provide forearm coverage that nitrile gloves don't offer at all. Neither replaces nitrile for chemical handling, but both are more practical for everyday planting and weeding.

### Can I use either of these gloves for rose pruning?

Neither is suitable for serious thorn work. Both have minimal puncture resistance. For rose pruning, look at gauntlet-style gloves with a reinforced palm and extended leather cuff, such as the Bionic Relief Grip or similar heavy-duty options. These two gloves are light-duty by design.

![Nitrile Garden Gloves — photo by ](/images/articles/hand-tools-7.jpg)


### How do I wash these gloves without damaging them?

Both are machine washable. For the Pine Tree Tools bamboo gloves, cold water on a gentle cycle and air drying will preserve the coating longest. For the Foxgloves, the same applies. Avoid high heat in the dryer for either, as it accelerates wear on the grip coating and can affect elasticity in the spandex blend.

### The Pine Tree Tools gloves say they run small. How should I size?

Order one size up if you're at the upper end of a size range. If you normally wear a medium and your hands are on the broader side, order large. The bamboo does give slightly with wear, but starting in a glove that's too snug will be uncomfortable and may reduce grip performance. Check the current reviews on the Amazon listing for the most recent sizing feedback, as the fit can vary between production runs.

### Are the Foxgloves worth the extra cost over the Pine Tree Tools?

They serve a different function, so comparing them purely on price isn't especially useful. If forearm protection matters to you, the Foxgloves are the only option of the two. If it doesn't, the Pine Tree Tools gloves are the more practical daily-use choice at a slightly lower price point. Buying both and using each for appropriate tasks gives you better coverage than either glove alone, and the combined cost is still under $40.
