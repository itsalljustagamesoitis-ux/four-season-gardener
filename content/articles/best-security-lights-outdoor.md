Most outdoor security lights fall into one of two categories: cheap solar units that underperform by October, or hardwired fixtures that require a licensed electrician and a permit before you can feel safe walking to your car at night. Neither extreme is particularly helpful. After running various combinations of motion-activated and landscape lighting across my 12 acres in Litchfield County, I've settled on four products that actually hold up across seasons and don't require a leap of faith. This is what I'd recommend to a capable adult who wants reliable outdoor lighting without the usual headaches.

For more context on how security and decorative lighting work together across a property, the [Garden Lighting](/lighting/) section of this site covers the broader picture. The picks below are the specific products I'd put money on.

## Top Picks

### Mr Beams Solar Wedge Plus 102 LED Security Outdoor Wall Light

**Best for: wireless installation with no electrical work**

[Mr Beams Solar Wedge Plus 102 LED Security Outdoor Wall Light](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07W464JPY?tag=fourseasong-20) runs around $35 to $40 at the time of writing, which puts it firmly in the budget category, though it performs above its price point in the daylight hours when the panel is charging. The 102-LED array produces 410 lumens of motion-activated light, which is brighter than most solar wall lights at this price by a meaningful margin. The built-in solar panel sits on top of the unit, so there's no separate panel to mount, cable, or angle. You put the light where you want it and the panel follows.

Installation is four screws. If you can read a level and hold a drill, you're done in fifteen minutes. There's no junction box, no conduit, no electrician to schedule. For renters, for outbuildings without electrical service, or for anyone who wants a security light on a detached garage without committing to a wiring project, this is the most practical option on the list.

The tradeoff is placement. The panel needs direct sun exposure to stay charged, which limits you to south or west-facing walls without significant shade. If your back fence line runs under a canopy of mature oaks, this unit won't perform well in July, let alone November. And there's no smart home integration, so you're working entirely with the built-in motion sensor and a fixed duration timer.

**Pros.**
- 410 lumens from 102 LEDs is brighter than comparable solar wall lights at this price

- No wiring whatsoever. Mounts with included screws in minutes
- Self-contained design means no separate solar panel to position

**Cons.**
- Only works reliably on walls with adequate daily sun exposure
- No compatibility with smart home systems or external controls

### LEONLITE 3CCT LED Security Light with Motion Sensor, Adjustable 3-Head

**Best for: permanent hardwired installation with serious output**

If you have an existing outdoor junction box or you're willing to have one installed, the [LEONLITE 3CCT LED Security Light with Motion Sensor, Adjustable 3-Head](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0C4PD4LLJ?tag=fourseasong-20) is the light I'd put over a driveway or garage entrance. Currently around $45 to $55, it's the most capable hardwired option at a budget price point.

Three independently adjustable heads give you genuine coverage flexibility. Fixed dual-head lights often leave a dead zone at a corner or an awkward angle where the second head just misses the gate. With three heads, you can address a wider arc or concentrate coverage on two specific zones simultaneously. The three selectable color temperatures (3000K warm, 4000K neutral, 5000K daylight) let you match the fixture to whatever else is already installed without a second trip to the electrical supply house.

The ETL listing is the specification I'd pay attention to here. ETL certification means the product has been independently tested against ANSI and UL safety standards, and it matters more than the waterproofing rating for a hardwired fixture. The IP65 weatherproofing is solid for a Connecticut winter or a Pacific Northwest wet season, but the ETL mark is the safety credential that tells you the electrical components meet North American standards.

Compared to a solar option, the difference is consistency. No battery degradation, no winter shortfalls when days drop below seven hours of sun, no capacity fade after three seasons. The light is as bright in February as it was in June. (That said, you do need a junction box, and if you don't have one, factor in the cost of an electrician before comparing total prices.)

**Pros.**
- Three selectable color temperatures in one fixture: 3000K, 4000K, 5000K
- Three independently adjustable heads cover a wider area than fixed dual-head models
- ETL listed and IP65 rated for hardwired permanent installation

**Cons.**
- Requires an existing outdoor junction box or electrician time to install one
- No smart home integration

**Best for: pathway and border coverage without any wiring**

The [LITOM 30 LED Solar Motion Sensor Landscape Spotlights, 4-Pack](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B075GQ54CY?tag=fourseasong-20) runs around $30 to $40 for the full pack at time of writing, which makes each spotlight roughly $8 to $10. At that price, the IP67 waterproof rating is the specification worth stopping on. IP65 means protected against water jets. IP67 means the unit can be submerged in up to one meter of water for thirty minutes. For a ground-level spotlight in a location that sees spring flooding, aggressive sprinkler overspray, or standing water after heavy rain, IP67 is the more meaningful rating. Most solar spotlights at this price are IP65 at best.

The three lighting modes add practical flexibility. Motion-activated mode is the obvious use case. Permanent-on dim mode works as a low-level pathway marker that runs through the night without draining the battery as fast. Permanent-on bright mode gives you full output continuously, though battery life will depend heavily on how much charge the panel accumulated that day. For a front path that needs consistent visibility rather than motion-triggered bursts, the dim mode is the setting I'd use as the default.

The four-unit pack covers a full front walkway or a perimeter of modest garden beds at a single purchase price, which is the real argument for this kit over individual spotlights. If you've been looking at similar options for purely decorative purposes, the approach is similar to what I covered with [garden fairy solar lights](/garden-fairy-solar-lights/), though the LITOM units are built around security output rather than ambiance.

The limitation to flag is the non-replaceable built-in battery. After several years of charge cycles, the cells will degrade and runtime will shorten noticeably. That's not a reason to avoid the product at this price point, but it's worth knowing you're buying a product with a finite lifespan rather than a permanent fixture.

- Three modes: motion-activated, permanent-on dim, permanent-on bright
- 4-pack covers an entire front pathway at one price

**Cons.**
- Built-in battery is not replaceable and will degrade after several years of use
- Placement restricted to locations with adequate sun exposure

**Best for: permanent landscape lighting that won't need replacing in five years**

The [VOLT Landscape Lighting Starter Kit with 12V Transformer and 8 Brass Spotlights](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B07CBV7HVW?tag=fourseasong-20) is the expensive option on this list, currently around $280 to $320 depending on where you find it. It is also the only option here that I'd describe as a one-time purchase. The brass fixtures do not rust, corrode, or degrade. The 12V low-voltage system is the same specification used by professional landscape contractors. The transformer includes a timer with an integrated photocell, so the lights come on at dusk and go off at dawn without any app, hub, or phone involved.

![Best Security Lights Outdoor](/images/articles/lighting-4.jpg)


VOLT sells direct to consumers, which is where the pricing lands. The same brass fixtures through a landscape contractor typically run two to three times higher once you add the markup and installation labor. At around $280 for eight spotlights plus the transformer, you're buying professional-grade material at roughly half the installed cost you'd see on a contractor estimate, though you're doing the wire splicing yourself.

Installation is more involved than any solar option on this list. You'll run low-voltage cable through your garden beds, splice each fixture to the cable using the included connectors, and run the main cable back to the transformer. It's not technically difficult, but it takes an afternoon and some planning. Plastic landscape kits from Malibu or Hampton Bay are faster to install, cheaper upfront, and will need replacing within a few seasons as the plastic fittings crack and the nickel-plated brass corrodes.

The honest comparison is solar versus permanent. A solar kit like the LITOM 4-pack is simpler, lighter on labor, and useful for areas without power access. VOLT is for the part of your property where you're committing to a permanent installation, the front beds, the main path to the door, the planting that frames the entrance. If that's what you're planning, buying plastic now to replace it with brass later costs more in the long run. (I made this mistake with my east border beds in 2019, which I mention only because it was avoidable.)

**Pros.**
- Solid brass fixtures will not corrode, rust, or crack regardless of ground conditions
- Professional-grade 12V system. The same spec used by landscape contractors
- Transformer timer with photocell automates the lights with no app or smart home hub required

**Cons.**

## Buying Guide

### Solar vs. Hardwired: The Actual Tradeoff

The choice between solar and hardwired isn't about which technology is superior. It's about what your property can support. If you have existing outdoor junction boxes and consistent power to outbuildings, hardwired options like the LEONLITE give you reliability that solar cannot match over multiple winters. Output doesn't vary by season, batteries don't degrade, and a motion light that needs to cover a high-traffic driveway will perform consistently in December.

![Best Security Lights Outdoor](/images/articles/lighting-5.jpg)


If you're working with locations where running electrical service would require trenching, permitting, or significant contractor time, solar is not a compromise position. The Mr Beams unit at $35 delivers real security light output from a four-screw installation. The LITOM 4-pack provides pathway coverage at roughly $10 per fixture. The limitation is sun exposure and battery longevity, not performance on a well-charged summer day.

### Waterproofing Ratings

IP65 means the fixture is protected against water jets from any direction. IP67 means it can be submerged briefly. For wall-mounted security lights, IP65 is sufficient. For ground-level spotlights in areas that see standing water, flooding, or aggressive irrigation, the LITOM's IP67 rating is the relevant specification. The difference matters most in spring, when the ground is saturated and your spotlights are sitting in the middle of it.

### Lumens and Coverage Area

410 lumens from the Mr Beams unit covers a reasonable driveway or entry area for motion detection. For a large parking area or a property perimeter, multiple units positioned with overlapping coverage will outperform a single higher-lumen fixture. The LEONLITE's three-head design helps here because the heads adjust independently rather than covering a fixed arc.

For landscape spotlights like the LITOM or VOLT units, lumen output is less important than beam angle and fixture placement. A 150-lumen spot aimed at a planting or path marker will do its job regardless of what the maximum lumen rating says on the box.

### When to Invest in Permanent Fixtures

If you're reading this list and reaching for the cheapest option to solve an immediate problem, that's reasonable. If you're planning a garden renovation, adding a patio, or laying out a front approach that you intend to keep for a decade, the VOLT kit is the right starting point. Landscape lighting done once in brass and low-voltage cable does not need to be redone. Plastic landscape lighting done twice costs more than brass done once. For broader ideas on how to plan a full property lighting scheme, the [outdoor lighting planning section](/lighting/) of this site is worth reading before you buy anything in quantity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many lumens do I need for a security light?

For motion-activated security over a standard driveway or entry area, 300 to 500 lumens is a practical range. The Mr Beams unit at 410 lumens handles a single-car driveway or garage entrance without issue. Larger areas, loading zones, or properties where you want to identify a person at distance benefit from 800 to 1500 lumens, which moves you into commercial-grade fixtures beyond this list.

### Can solar security lights work through winter?

In climates with short winter days and significant cloud cover, solar lights will charge more slowly and run shorter cycles than in summer. The Mr Beams and LITOM units will still function in winter, but motion-triggered duration and brightness may decrease in December and January compared to June. If consistent winter performance is a priority, the LEONLITE hardwired option removes the variable entirely.

### Do I need an electrician to install a hardwired security light?

If you have an existing outdoor junction box with a weatherproof cover, most homeowners can replace or install a hardwired fixture themselves. If no outdoor junction box exists, running new electrical service from your panel requires a licensed electrician in most jurisdictions. Factor that cost into the total price when comparing hardwired against solar options.

### What is the difference between IP65 and IP67 waterproofing?

IP65 means the fixture is sealed against low-pressure water jets from any direction, which covers rain, sprinkler spray, and garden hose overspray. IP67 adds protection against submersion in up to one meter of water for thirty minutes. For wall-mounted lights, IP65 is adequate. For ground-level spotlights in areas prone to standing water or flooding, IP67 is the more reliable rating.

### Is low-voltage landscape lighting safe for DIY installation?

Yes. The VOLT 12V system operates at low voltage and does not present the same risks as line-voltage electrical work. The transformer steps down standard 120V household current to 12V before it reaches the fixtures and cable. You can splice fixtures, run cable, and connect to the transformer without electrical credentials. The transformer plugs into a standard outdoor outlet. The only part of the system that operates at line voltage is inside the transformer enclosure, which you do not open or modify.
