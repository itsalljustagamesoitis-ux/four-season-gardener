If you've spent any time comparing outdoor dining furniture, you already know the drill: real teak looks beautiful, costs a fortune, and demands annual maintenance. Cheaper alternatives look like outdoor furniture for about three seasons before they start showing it. The [POLYWOOD Nautical Trestle 7-Piece Dining Set in Teak](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B085G5FVR5?tag=fourseasong-20) sits in a third category that doesn't get enough credit. It's made from high-density polyethylene lumber, costs significantly less than a genuine teak dining set, and requires essentially nothing from you once it's assembled. This review covers whether it actually delivers on that promise, and whether the premium price over basic aluminum or entry-level sets is justified.

For a broader look at what we test and recommend, the site's [Outdoor Furniture](/outdoor-furniture/) hub is a good starting point if you're still in the research phase.

## Quick Verdict

Buy this if you want the look of teak outdoor dining furniture without the maintenance obligations of actual teak, and you're willing to pay more upfront for something that will still look the same in fifteen years. Pass on it if you need a set you can move frequently, if your budget tops out around $800, or if you're furnishing a smaller space where a 73-inch round table would dominate.

The POLYWOOD Nautical Trestle 7-Piece is currently around $1,850 to $2,100 on Amazon, depending on timing. That's real money. It is also, if you're comparing against a genuine teak dining set (which runs $3,000 to $8,000 and requires annual oiling), a more defensible number than it first appears.

## What We Tested

The set includes a 73-inch round table with a trestle base and six dining chairs. POLYWOOD's teak-colored HDPE lumber is made from recycled plastic. The material does not absorb moisture, will not rot or splinter, and carries a 20-year residential warranty. The teak color finish is a warm medium brown with visible wood-grain texture pressed into the surface.

![Round Teak Outdoor Dining Set — photo by ](/images/articles/outdoor-furniture-6.jpg)


Assembly is straightforward but time-consuming. Budget two to three hours for the full set if you're working alone, less with a second person on the table base. Hardware quality is adequate. Nothing felt cheap, but the process isn't something you'd describe as enjoyable.

The table itself is substantial. At 73 inches across, it seats six adults comfortably with room for serving dishes in the center. The trestle base is stable under load. Chairs have a slight backward rake that reads as relaxed rather than slouched, and the seat depth is workable for longer dinners without becoming uncomfortable.

Weight is a real factor here. The table is heavy enough that repositioning it is a two-person job. The chairs are individually manageable, but this is not a set you'll be casually moving around your patio to follow the shade. Position it where you want it and expect it to live there.

## Performance

### Weather Resistance

HDPE lumber handles freeze-thaw cycles, wet springs, and sustained sun exposure without the problems that plague real wood. No cracking, no warping, no seasonal storage requirements. After a hard winter, you wipe it down and it looks the same as it did in September. I've seen real teak sets left out over multiple winters develop gray patina and surface checks that require sanding before re-oiling. The POLYWOOD avoids all of that, which is not a small thing if you'd rather spend your spring doing something other than furniture maintenance.

Color stability in direct sun is good. POLYWOOD's HDPE holds its teak tone better than powder-coated aluminum holds a painted finish over time. Some competing sets in this color family, including aluminum-framed options from brands like Modway and Vifah, start showing fade or coating wear within a few seasons of full sun exposure. The HDPE material fades more slowly and doesn't peel.

### Comfort and Usability

The chairs are functional without being exceptional. Seat height is standard at around 18 inches. The back support is decent for a dining chair that's doing double duty at backyard dinners and weekend lunches. If you're planning extended outdoor entertaining, you might add seat cushions. POLYWOOD sells cushion options designed for this set, or standard 18-inch outdoor chair pads work as well. Expect to pay $30 to $60 per chair for cushions if you go that route.

The round table format is genuinely useful for six-person dining. Conversation flows better around a round table than a rectangle at this scale, and the 73-inch diameter means no one is reaching awkwardly. There's no center umbrella hole on this model, which is worth noting if shade coverage is a priority for your space. A freestanding cantilever umbrella is the solution, and quality options start around $200 to $350.

### Durability and Long-Term Value

This is where the POLYWOOD's price starts to make more sense. A comparable genuine teak round outdoor dining set, like the ones sold by brands such as TeakCraft or Kingsley Bate, runs $4,000 to $7,000. It requires teak oil or sealer applied at least once per year, more if you're in a wet climate. Neglect that maintenance and the wood grays, checks, and eventually deteriorates. With the POLYWOOD, the total cost of ownership over ten years is essentially the purchase price. No oil, no sealer, no refinishing, no replacement.

For people who've owned real teak and understand what they're buying out of, the POLYWOOD's value argument is clear. For buyers coming from cheap resin or aluminum sets, the sticker price requires more justification. At $1,900-plus, you're paying for longevity and aesthetic quality that entry sets can't match. Whether that math works depends entirely on how long you plan to stay in your current home and how much you value not thinking about your patio furniture.

## Pros and Cons

**What works:**

- No maintenance required. Genuinely none. Hose it off when it gets dirty.
- 73-inch round table seats six adults with room for food and drinks in the center.
- 20-year residential warranty means POLYWOOD stands behind the durability claims.
- Teak color and wood-grain texture read convincingly as real wood from any reasonable distance.
- HDPE construction eliminates every weather-related failure mode that affects wood furniture.

**What doesn't:**

- Heavy. The table is a two-person lift, and the set's position is effectively permanent once you place it.
- No umbrella hole. A cantilever umbrella solves this but adds to the total cost.
- The price. At $1,900 to $2,100, it's a harder sell if you're not thinking in ten-year terms.
- Chair comfort is adequate, not outstanding. Cushions help but aren't included.

## Who Should Buy This

The POLYWOOD Nautical Trestle set makes the most sense for a specific type of buyer. If you've owned real teak before and found the maintenance cycle frustrating, this is a direct answer to that problem. If you're furnishing a property you intend to hold for a long time and want outdoor furniture that won't be a recurring expense or project, the math favors this set over cheaper alternatives you'll replace in five years.

If you entertain regularly for groups of six and want a table that handles the occasion without visual apology, the 73-inch round format at this quality level does that.

It's not the right set if your patio layout requires flexible furniture placement. The weight is a real constraint. It's also not the right choice if your current priority is getting an outdoor dining space set up at moderate cost, with the intention of upgrading later. For that situation, the money is better spent elsewhere, and there are decent mid-range aluminum sets from brands like Crosley or Hanover that will serve adequately for a few seasons.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does POLYWOOD teak color compare to real teak in appearance?

From a normal viewing distance, reasonably well. The pressed wood-grain texture in POLYWOOD's HDPE surface adds visual credibility, and the warm medium-brown teak color is accurate to freshly oiled real teak. Up close, you can tell it's not wood. The surface has a slightly different quality than natural grain. Whether that matters depends on how closely your guests inspect your furniture.

![Round Teak Outdoor Dining Set — photo by ](/images/articles/outdoor-furniture-2.jpg)


### Does the POLYWOOD Nautical Trestle set need any maintenance?

No meaningful maintenance. POLYWOOD recommends occasional cleaning with soap and water or a mild household cleaner. No oiling, no sealing, no sanding, no seasonal storage required. If mold or mildew develops in very humid conditions, a diluted bleach solution cleans it off. That's it.

### Can this set stay outside year-round?

Yes, and that's the practical case for buying it. HDPE lumber is unaffected by moisture, freezing temperatures, and UV exposure in ways that matter. Leaving it out over winter is fine. POLYWOOD's 20-year residential warranty covers the material against fading and structural failure. If you want to cover it with patio furniture covers during extended periods of non-use, that's fine, but it's not required.

### What's the weight capacity for the chairs?

POLYWOOD rates the dining chairs in this set at 300 pounds per chair. The table's trestle base is built for stability under load, and the overall construction is solid. This is not a concern for most buyers, but it's a real specification if it's relevant to your situation.

### Is this set worth the price compared to a real teak dining set?

If you're comparing against a quality genuine teak set, yes. A teak set of comparable size from a reputable manufacturer runs $3,500 to $7,000, requires annual maintenance, and will show neglect if you skip it. The POLYWOOD costs less upfront and nothing afterward. If you're comparing against a $700 aluminum or resin set, the calculation is different. You're paying a premium for longevity and aesthetics that cheaper materials won't match over ten years, but the upfront gap is real and only makes sense if you're thinking in those terms.
