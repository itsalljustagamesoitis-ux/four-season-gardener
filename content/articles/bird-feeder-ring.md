If you've spent any time trying to keep squirrels out of a bird feeder, you already know how that usually goes. Baffles get climbed. Cages get forced. Motors burn out. The squirrel wins, and you're back on Amazon at 11pm looking at the next solution. The [Squirrel Buster Plus Bird Feeder with Cardinal Ring](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0007LQ3RQ?tag=fourseasong-20) from Brome takes a different approach: pure mechanical weight exclusion, no moving parts beyond the cage itself, no batteries, no charging. I've been running one for two full seasons now, and it's the first squirrel-proof feeder I'd actually recommend without qualifications. More on that below, but if you're browsing options in the [Bird Feeders & Baths](/birds-wildlife/) category and you're serious about squirrel exclusion, this is the one to understand first.

## Quick Verdict

The [Squirrel Buster Plus with Cardinal Ring](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0007LQ3RQ?tag=fourseasong-20) runs around $55 to $65 on Amazon at the time of writing, depending on availability and seller. For a passive, mechanical squirrel exclusion system with a 5.1 lb seed capacity and cardinal ring accommodation, that's fair. The Droll Yankees Flipper runs closer to $90 to $100 and requires four D batteries. The Brome needs nothing but seed and an appropriate hang point.

This is the feeder I'd buy if I were starting from scratch. If you've got an existing pole setup and want a baffle-only solution, read our piece on [squirrel domes for bird feeders](/squirrel-dome-for-bird-feeder/) instead. But if you want a complete feeder that handles the squirrel problem on its own, the Squirrel Buster Plus is the answer I keep coming back to.

## What We Tested

I hung the Squirrel Buster Plus on a 7/8-inch diameter shepherd's hook pole in a semi-open area of my property, about 12 feet from the nearest tree line. That distance matters, though the feeder's design would still handle a squirrel that managed the drop from a branch above. I ran it through spring, summer, and into fall, which gave me wet weather, dry spells, and heavy squirrel pressure from a resident gray squirrel population that I can only describe as persistent.

![Bird Feeder Ring — photo by ](/images/articles/birds-wildlife-8.jpg)


The cardinal ring is the version-specific feature worth naming. It's a circular wire perch that extends outward from the base of the feeder, giving larger birds like northern cardinals a stable landing platform while smaller birds use the standard ports. The base ring also happens to be what keeps the whole system from excluding birds you actually want to feed.

The feeder holds approximately 5.1 lbs of mixed seed or black oil sunflower, which across six feeding ports gives you reasonable capacity without the mess of an overfilled tube. I loaded it with straight black oil sunflower for most of the test period.

## Performance

### Squirrel Exclusion

This is the only thing that actually matters for a feeder in this category, so I'll be direct. The weight-activated mechanism works. A squirrel grabs the cage or the cardinal ring, the outer shroud drops under their weight, and the seed ports are blocked. The squirrel gets nothing. They try again. They get nothing again. After a few days, most of them stop trying consistently, though I had one particularly determined animal who kept at it for about two weeks before redirecting elsewhere.

The key adjustment is the weight sensitivity dial, which lets you set a threshold between roughly 3 oz and 12 oz. Set it too low and you'll accidentally exclude large birds. Set it too high and you let squirrels through. Getting it right takes a couple of sessions of watching who's landing on the feeder and whether the cage drops prematurely for birds you want. (I timed this at about 20 minutes of actual observation over two mornings, which I realize is a specific complaint, but it's worth knowing going in.)

### Bird Access and the Cardinal Ring

Without the cardinal ring, the Squirrel Buster Plus accommodates standard perching birds fine but cardinals often struggle. They're large, they prefer a stable platform, and the narrow ports on a standard tube feeder aren't their first choice. The ring gives them a perch and access to the lower ports in a way that actually held cardinals at my feeder consistently from April through September.

![Bird Feeder Ring — photo by ](/images/articles/birds-wildlife-1.jpg)


I tracked visits informally, not scientifically, and saw regular activity from cardinals, chickadees, nuthatches, house finches, and goldfinches. A pair of hairy woodpeckers tried it occasionally but didn't stick around. The ring isn't designed for woodpeckers and they seemed to agree.

The grackle exclusion feature is worth understanding separately. If you dial the weight sensitivity low enough, common grackles and European starlings will trigger the cage closure the same way squirrels do. For many feeders, that's exactly what they want. If you have grackles stripping your feeder in an afternoon, being able to exclude them without excluding cardinals is a real capability. It takes some dialing, but it works.

### Durability and Weather Performance

Two seasons of hard use, including freezing temperatures that dropped well below zero for several nights running, wet springs with ice formation, and summer heat in the 90s, produced no mechanical failures. The spring tension on the shroud remained consistent throughout. The plastic components on the ports showed no cracking or UV degradation I could see.

Cleaning is the one area where I'd push back on anyone who calls this feeder low-maintenance. It takes apart for cleaning, which is good, but there are more components than a simple tray feeder or open hopper. If you clean feeders every two weeks the way you should (most people don't, and they should), plan for about 10 to 15 minutes per session, which compares unfavorably to a tray feeder I could rinse in two. That's the honest tradeoff for the squirrel mechanism.

## Pros and Cons

**Pros**

- Weight-activated exclusion requires no batteries or motors. Nothing to charge, nothing to replace, nothing that burns out in cold weather
- 5.1 lb capacity with six ports is generous for a tube-style squirrel-proof feeder
- Adjustable weight threshold lets you exclude squirrels, grackles, or starlings depending on where you set it
- Cardinal ring meaningfully improves access for large birds without compromising the exclusion mechanism
- Brome's customer service and parts availability are genuinely good; I've ordered replacement shroud components for other Brome feeders in the past without difficulty

**Cons**

- Cleaning involves more disassembly than simpler feeders. Plan for it
- Weight sensitivity requires calibration. It's not plug-and-play if you want to dial it precisely
- The cardinal ring adds bulk. If you're hanging in a tight space or want something visually minimal, this isn't it

## Who Should Buy This

If you've tried baffles and found squirrels still getting through because they're dropping from above, or if you've run through multiple battery-powered exclusion feeders and are tired of the maintenance cycle, the Squirrel Buster Plus is a practical step forward. You can read more about baffle-only approaches in our guide to [bird feeder baffles for squirrels](/bird-feeder-baffles-for-squirrels/), but the Brome handles the problem at the feeder itself, which simplifies the whole setup.

The cardinal ring version specifically makes sense if you're actively trying to attract cardinals. If you're indifferent to cardinals or your property doesn't have them, the standard Squirrel Buster Plus (without the ring) is a few dollars cheaper and functionally identical in every other way.

This feeder also suits anyone who wants to hang from an existing hook or shepherd's crook rather than invest in a dedicated feeder pole system. It's not designed for deck-rail mounting; if that's your situation, a [bird feeder for deck](/bird-feeder-for-deck/) setup would serve you better. The Squirrel Buster Plus wants to hang free, ideally at least 8 feet from any surface a squirrel could use as a launch point, though the weight mechanism will catch them even if they drop directly from above.

![Bird Feeder Ring — photo by ](/images/articles/birds-wildlife-3.jpg)


Experienced feeders who have already optimized pole placement and baffling and are looking for an additional mechanical layer will find this redundant, though I'd argue it simplifies the whole system to consolidate exclusion into the feeder itself. If you're setting up a feeding station from scratch and want one feeder that handles squirrels without ongoing attention, the Squirrel Buster Plus is where I'd put the money.

One honest note on expectations: no squirrel-proof feeder is squirrel-proof forever if a squirrel is determined and clever enough. What the Brome does is make the effort unrewarding consistently enough that most squirrels give up. That's a more accurate description of "squirrel-proof" than most marketing will give you.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does the Squirrel Buster Plus cardinal ring differ from the standard model?

The cardinal ring is an extended circular perch attached to the base of the feeder. It gives larger birds, particularly northern cardinals, a stable landing platform to access the lower seed ports. The standard Squirrel Buster Plus without the ring works identically for squirrel exclusion, but cardinals and other large-bodied birds have a harder time using it comfortably. If cardinals are a priority, the ring version is worth the small price premium.

### What weight setting should I use to exclude squirrels but still allow cardinals?

This depends on your individual birds and squirrels, which is an honest answer but also the correct one. A northern cardinal typically weighs between 1.5 and 1.7 oz. A gray squirrel weighs anywhere from 14 to 21 oz. In practice, setting the weight threshold somewhere around 3 to 4 oz allows cardinals and excludes squirrels with a comfortable margin. If grackles or starlings are also a problem, you can tighten the threshold to around 2 to 3 oz, but test it with live birds before walking away.

![Bird Feeder Ring — photo by ](/images/articles/birds-wildlife-4.jpg)


### Is the Squirrel Buster Plus difficult to clean?

More involved than a tray or simple hopper feeder, less complicated than some tube feeders with fixed components. The shroud, tube, and ports all separate for cleaning. Plan for about 10 to 15 minutes if you're being thorough. Brome recommends cleaning every two weeks during active use, and that schedule is worth following to prevent mold in wet seed and disease transmission among birds.

### Will the Squirrel Buster Plus work if squirrels are dropping onto it from a tree above?

Yes. The weight-activated closure triggers regardless of the direction of approach. Whether a squirrel climbs up from below or drops from a branch above, their weight on the cage or ring triggers the shroud and closes the ports. Pole placement away from jumping-off points still reduces traffic, but the mechanism handles aerial approaches.

### How does the Squirrel Buster Plus compare to the Droll Yankees Flipper?

The Droll Yankees Flipper runs around $90 to $100 and uses a motor powered by four D batteries to spin the perch ring when a squirrel lands, throwing them off. It's effective and entertaining to watch, but it has a motor that can fail in cold weather and batteries that need replacing. The Brome uses no batteries or motors. It's around $55 to $65, requires no power source, and the mechanism is less likely to fail over time. If you want the entertainment factor, the Flipper delivers. If you want passive, long-term exclusion without maintenance, the Brome wins on practicality.
